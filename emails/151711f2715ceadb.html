CANAVARRO, NUNO "Plux Quba" (Drag City) lp 19.98

Plux Quba is a record that after the nearly 30 years of its existence remains impossible to categorize. It's almost as if it was an alien broadcast beamed in from another planet. Little is known about its creator, Nuno Canavarro, outside of his native Portugal, and its discovery is so unbelievably legendary and riddled with hearsay that it's easy to dismiss this as a probable hoax.

Recorded in 1988 and released on a private label, it was believed to be discovered in 1991 by Jim O'Rourke while traveling through Europe with Jan St. Werner (Microstoria, Mouse On Mars) and Carsten Schulz (C-Shultz and Hajsch). Liking what he heard, Rourke eventually started a label call Moikai in 1998 and the first release was Plux Quba remastered by Portuguese guitarist and composer Rafael Toral. That the sounds contained on Plux Quba would go on to heavily influence the sound of all three artists' later output (not to mention bearing a strong sonic resemblance to Aphex Twin's early ambient pieces, Boards of Canada's nostalgic filmstrip melodies, Prefuse 73's glitch-hop, and Christian Fennesz's sublime Endless Summer) is a bizarre case of cosmic synchronicity. Rumors that this was an elaborate prank by the three electronic artists have since been quelled by enough evidence of Canavarro's existence as an architectural student, a member of semi-popular Portuguese new wave bands, and his subsequent compositional work in Portuguese cinema.

Indeed, the 15 tracks (8 of which are untitled - many clock in just over a minute) of Plux Quba can be seen as a bridge between the electro-acoustic computer-processing works of Robert Ashley (1979's Automatic Writing) and David Behrman (1984's Leapday Night) on the Lovely music label, and the advent of laptop-based electronica of the nineties. Yet Plux Quba seems less aware than that, as if created in a total vacuum. Its mystery remains one of its key attractions.

I (Scott) was turned on to this by friend and Alps band-mate Jefre Cantu-Ledesma in the nineties when he dubbed it to cassette for me, after raving about it endlessly. At first, I didn't understand the appeal. It doesn't endear itself to the listener right away. Each track is its own micro-universe of texture and mood. The first bursts of high piercing squelches, random cluster tones, bell drones and squeals, come off as abrasive and abstract, appear than disappear. Its soft-volume minimal experiments are heightened by pauses of pregnant silence that are not easy on casual listeners. It's not until about five short tracks in that processed disembodied voices, bird-like chirps and chord washes begin to emote a tangible melancholy melody, and that's when Plux Quba begins to work its magic. It took so many concentrated listens to that tape before I was finally under its spell.

Made with electronics, melodica, and pre-recorded tapes of acoustic instruments such as harp, flute, bells, marimbas, organ, an out of tune toy piano, and accordion, the overall sound is augmented by whispered transmitted voices, abstracted squeals, glitchy computerized electronics, toy instruments, crying children, animal noises, Conet Project style shortwave babble, and off-kilter hand percussion. It's almost as if a computer was fed some primitive bedroom recordings performed by children and asked to mimic the results. The cover art with its childlike drawing and strange font layouts doesn't offer any clues either, unless one is versed in Portuguese perhaps. But even then, Plux Quba seems to be mining a territory where language is useless as an orienting factor, but trades on a highly prevalent universality of emotion. It remains a singular and beguiling artifact that won't appeal to everybody, but offers massive rewards to the curious, adventurous and patient.

AVENGERS, THE "s/t" (Superior Viaduct) lp 17.98

Reissued by Water a few years ago, now again on vinyl by the unstoppable Superior Viaduct, one of THEE greatest punk rock records ever. As a band, these legendary SF punks existed for a scant two years, and only released an ep before they broke up; their debut lp, this one right here, was only released in 1983, four years AFTER the band disbanded, but it displayed an incredible knack for writing amazing pop songs, and hnted at what could have been, had the Avengers survived to make another record.

And if "We Are The One" isn't already enough, "Car Crash" will seal the deal, all super melodic bassline, and chugging churning riffs, dark and minor key, Houston's vocals all raspy and occasionally cracking, and the crazy catchy "wooooah oooh oooh" chorus that you'll find stuck in your head forEVER.

BRETSCHNEIDER, FRANK "Isolation" (Line) cd 14.98

Bretschneider is long recognized as one of the founding engineers of the swaggering post-techno rhythms of the seminal Raster-Noton label, alongside Alva Noto, Byetone, Senking, etc. Isolation eschews the rhythmic structure but not the sine-tone and white noise instrumentation for this ghastly piece of minimalism originally scored as an installation in a former Stasi prison in Bautzen in the former East Germany. Sensory deprivation and severe solitude were common practices on the general prison population, and Bretschneider directs his piercing tones and thrumming low-end, embedding the acoustic phenomenon of the anechoic chamber (where the extreme high pitches of the human nervous system and the extreme low frequencies of blood flowing through the body become audible) into the psychologically debilitating conditions of a brutal incarceration. As grim as the subject matter is, Bretschneider's work is a somber meditation on these themes, all done with exemplary skill and technique akin to the lowercase work of Bernard Gunter, Richard Chartier, etc.

COPPICE "Matches" (Category Of Manifestation) cd 14.98

A prepared pump organ and shruti box are amongst the chief instruments for the electro-acoustic duo Coppice, who have yet to exhaust the possibilities of these tools in their poking and prodding amongst the bellows, keys, and rudimentary mechanics. Tape manipulation and hard-disc computer edits are found within their compositions, but much of the work is recorded and developed in a live context, giving their wheezing drones, gasping bellows, soft rhythmic unspooling, spring-loaded tension, and clattering wooden cranks an idiosyncratic tactility which serves to amplify the physical nature of these sounds. When the electronics do come into the fray, fractal patterns blossom in heavily computerized algorithms rotating, expanding, collapsing all at once. Akin to the work of Eric Cordier, Joe Colley, Small Cruel Party, and Francisco Meirino. Self-described rather cryptically as "a story with many holes", Matches is packaged with metal mesh, colored thread, and transparencies, and is available as a properly replicated cd in a numbered limited edition of just 100 copies.

HEY COLOSSUS "Radio Static High" (Rocket Recordings) lp 28.00

NOW ALSO ON VINYL!!!!

This out-there outfit from England, unclassifiable specialists in heaviness and krautiness and spaciness and other awesome-ness, wowed us earlier this year with an eclectic album entitled In Black And Gold, and now are back already, somehow, with another groovy album full of surprises, Radio Static High. Seriously, Hey Colossus are one of those rare bands who seem like they must have secretly broken into our homes and carefully studied our record collections before going into the studio and synthesizing a sound that brings a bunch of stuff we love together in unexpected ways. Certainly some Circle, a little Harvey Milk, a bunch of krautrock, a lot of '80s UK prog & post-punk... These ten tracks are filled with driving rhythmic pulsations, dubby psychedelic effects, new wave-y catchiness, fuzzed out riffage, post-rock moodiness, stomping distortion, and melody, yes melody - compared even to In Black And Gold, this seems to us to be the most melodic album Hey Colossus have yet made. Maybe it's the vocals, starting with the spaced-out stoner pop of the title track. Though the 'groove' here is also very, very significant, tracks like "Hop The Railings" sounding a lot like Circle. Then you've got the precision prog element, with the likes of "Snapping Undone" in the mode of Belew-era Crimson - but also sorta Circular. It's a pretty unusual & fantastic mix as far as we're concerned, making for another recommended release from these guys!

HOLCOMB, ROSCOE "San Diego State Folk Festival 1972" (Tompkins Square) lp 15.98

We still have a few of these left from Black Friday's Record Store Day offerings, one of the items we were most excited about, Tompkins Square's documentation of a rare West Coast appearance from Mr. High Lonesome himself, Roscoe Holcomb. Holcomb's unique and legendary mountain holler strikes audiences directly to the bone through dark and lonely songs that had been passed down through oral traditions for generations. A self-taught back-porch musician never keen on recording, but he always was a force to be reckoned with live. As an added bonus, the wonderful Jean Ritchie appears on one track too! Recommended. Get this before they're gone like the wind.

HOLTER, JULIA "Have You In My Wilderness" (Domino) cd 14.98

Julia Holter's dynamically percussive voice is simply hypnotic, lifting and lilting to the point of almost completely becoming one with the orchestral string arrangements which populate Have You In My Wilderness.

Between Ducktails, Beirut, Joanna Newsom, and this delightful masterpiece from Holter, 2015 seems to mark the rise of beautiful, ever-so-slightly experimental baroque pop. Balancing moments of explosiveness and reservedness, the album builds to a stunning finish in its title track. Singing of the battle between settling in love and seeking autonomy, Holter leaves us on the most perfect exhalation of sound. Don't miss this masterful release!

HOWLROUND (AKA ROBIN THE FOG) "Tales From The Black Tangle" (The Fog Signals) lp 16.98

We were recently fortunate enough to host a really intimate performance by Robin the Fog (one half of the duo Howlround) in the store, and despite it being a stripped down version of his normally quite intricate live setup (only two reel-to-reel machine, not the usual four or more), it still totally mesmerized everyone in the store. Dense, gorgeous, haunting, even downright menacing at times, Tales from the Black Tangle likewise engulfs you in dense soundscapes that recall the dronologists on Students of Decay or Belong's October Language. The twist here is that all the material on here is sourced from field recordings in abandoned buildings and such, many of them downright MUNDANE things like creaking doors and drawers. How he manages to conjure such beauty and vibrancy out of totally unprocessed sound sources is anyone's guess. We only got a few copies from the man himself, and as there were only 300 copies pressed, restocks are unlikely.

IL BALLETTO DI BRONZO "Ys" (Polydor) lp 35.00

One of our crazy '70s Italian prog faves now reissued on vinyl! Il Balletto Di Bronzo's nutzoid sci-fi keyboard attack will bring to mind ELP, but you'll also think of Magma (and their descendants Shub-Niggurath and Koenjihyakkei as well). Complex, powerful, heavy, precise yet darkly psychedelic, Ys (the band's second album, originally released in 1972) is one of those timeless pieces of rock n' roll art that's definitely very '70s yet not entirely dated: you could imagine one of today's math rock or heavy space rock bands like Mammatus or Hey Colossus kicking out these jams, that is, except for the occasional operatic vocal chorus. This album's five long songs, with their acid guitar action, haunting female vocals, droning keyboards, martial drumming, baroque/classical motifs, and weird changes, are just about as good as over-the-top Italian prog can get, and that's pretty damn good. A top ten prog album (if Aquarius had a prog top ten, and please don't ask us for one, we'll be arguing for weeks).

ILITCH "10 Suicides" (Superior Viaduct) lp 23.00

Album number two from Ilitch, originally released in 1980, reissued now on vinyl by the fine folks at Superior Viaduct, yay! 10 Suicides is a record of dark and claustrophobic, primitive and noisy electronic soundscapes utilizing vocoder, guitar, sequencer, synthesizer, bass, saxophone, piano, harmonium, noise, treatments, and vocals. The sound varies greatly (although the sinister vibe is always present): rumbling distorted drones with pitchshifted vocals and high pitched synthesizer melodies, gorgeous soundscapes with sparse minor key piano and distant sine waves, distorted and heavily reverbed rhythms with spacy and whiny vocals, heavily strummed guitar and distorted drum machine rhythms with noodly melodies over dark ominous synthscapes, pounding distorted proto punk with Joe-Meek-I-Hear-A-New-World style chipmunk vocals. While it's "poppier" than Ilitch's Periodikmindrouble debut, with some shades of what was to come on the coldwave, sythpop Ruth album in '85 (which we think SV is also planning to reissue, cool!), you can still think Heldon, or a more evil Eno & Fripp, and maybe Chris & Cosey too. Includes 16 page booklet, and download.

ILITCH "Periodikmindtrouble" (Superior Viaduct) lp 23.00

Rad, Superior Viaduct has picked these old aQ faves to reissue on vinyl, the first two albums from French guitarist/experimentalist Thierry Muller, aka Ilitch. On his debut Periodikmindtrouble, originally released in 1978, Ilitch makes use of "destructured" guitar, droning organ, harmonium, tape loops, synth, etc. to create dark, electronic soundscapes comparable to his fellow countrymen Heldon (whom Superior Viaduct have also done one reissue of) or a scarier Fripp & Eno. Experimental, atmospheric stuff indeed - primitive, eerie, abstract, sometimes violent! Trainspotters should note that Ilitch is one of the avant-garde obscurities that appeared on the notorious 'Nurse With Wound list' found on NWW's first album, along with much other seminal krautrock/noise/experimental weirdness, more and more of which is thankfully being reissued every day...

Gatefold sleeve, comes with download.

KLEISTWAHR "The Return" (Fourth Dimension) cd 15.98

Not only is there a killer new Ramleh double cd set on this week's list, but we also just got a first-time-on-cd edition of this, originally released on vinyl in Noiseville's awesome (and super limited) Outer Bounds Of Sound series in 2009, Kleistwahr being another project of legendary UK noise merchant Gary Mundy (Ramleh, Skullflower, Broken Flag collective) that fits nicely within the man's legacy of harsh electronic noise. LES RALLIZES DENUDES "Live 77" (Victory) 2lp 34.00

You hear that? That echoing, droning, heavy psych jamming destroying your mind? Once more, at long last reissued on vinyl, the distorto-delic '70s Japanese guitar psych holy grail known as Live '77, from perhaps THEE pioneering, influential underground Japanese psych outfit, Les Rallizes Denudes.

LIBRA "Schock" (AMS / Cinevox) lp 29.00

A vinyl reissue of this rare '70s Italian horror movie soundtrack (for cult director Mario Bava's 1977 film "Schock"), composed and performed by a very Goblin-like band called Libra. Fittingly for the murderous, paranormal goings-on of Bava's flick, this album is full of gothic keyboards, eerie wordless vocal choirs, queasy prog-funk synth vamps, and ominous drum-scapes. Libra really rock out on the Schock theme, while elsewhere they deal in creepy electronic ambience. Bands like Libra can make sweet lullabies seem scarier than full-on screams and metal guitars, and yet their spooky piano instrumentals wouldn't be out of place if used as intros for Cradle Of Filth songs. Soundtrack/horror/Goblin fans will be pleased.
