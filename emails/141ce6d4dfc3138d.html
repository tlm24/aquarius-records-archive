DAY OF THE DEAD (JOHN HARRISON) "OST" (Waxwork) 2lp 40.00

We just got this in, but wanted to include it on our Halloween /

horror vinyl themed in-between list, so we just may write more about

this on a future list, but for now, here are the basics: composer John

Harrison's iconic soundtrack to George Romero's legendary horror

zombie classic, Day Of The Dead, is the second release on Waxwork

after Re-Animator, and comes pressed on 180 gram vinyl, the first time

ever the COMPLETE score has been available, including EVERY musical

cue used in the film. Also of note about this release: the 1985 master

tapes were restored and remixed by former White Zombie guitarist, J.

Yuenger (!!).

The two lps are pressed on cream yellow marbled / 'military

green' marbled vinyl, the cover is a heavy old school tip-on sleeve,

with matte varnish, a full color gatefold and spot UV gloss printing,

the liner notes include an essay by Romero himself, and the whole

thing features all new art and design by artist Jay Shaw, PLUS,

there's also an 11" x 22" replica of "THE DEAD WALK!" newspaper

article from the opening credits of the film, which features notes on

the music from John Harrison. So cool!!!!

We had thought they were doing Rosemary's Baby next after Re-

Animator, still looking forward to that, but no complaints about this!

ESCAPE FROM NEW YORK (CARPENTER, JOHN) "OST" (Death Waltz) lp 29.00

Very cool, fancy import vinyl reissue of this Carpenter classic

(and onetime aQ Record Of The Week), from a mysterious UK label who

have just started doing a whole series of uber desirable soundtrack

reissues on lp. We're always going on about John Carpenter, citing his

music as a big influence on a lot of underground bands today, and this

is definitely a good example of what we love about his stuff.

Here's what we said about this when we made the cd reish a ROTW

back in '05:

Why are we making a soundtrack for a movie from 1983 starring

Kurt Russell (who hams it up in a piratical eye-patch) our Record Of

The Week, this week? Well, we've got a few reasons... first off, the

music is awesome and we love it (good reason right there). Secondly,

this cd has been out of print and hard to find for several years now,

going for silly sums on eBay, sought after by soundtrack collectors

and electronic music fans.

While director John Carpenter is known for making quite a few

classic films in the B-grade horror and sci-fi thriller genres

(Halloween, The Thing, Assault On Precinct 13, The Fog, They Live

etc.), it's perhaps not as well known that he personally composed the

soundtrack music to many of his movies, starting with his first flick

Dark Star back in 1974. Working out of a home studio with what was

technically advanced equipment at the time (machines which would now

be considered awesomely vintage), he composed electronic music

soundtracks that some might say are even better than the movies

themselves. Certainly they're worthy to stand on their own, anyway

(just like Goblin's soundtracks for Argento's films). Carpenter's

compositions have influenced other musicians, and not just soundtrack

writers. Anyone who loved the Zombi album we recommended last year

needs to have some John Carpenter in their collection for sure!

And we recommend Escape From New York as being one of his best.

Somehow it combines a lot of stuff that we figure a lot of you like:

suspenseful Goblinesque darkness, droning old school synths, kitchy

Disco Not Disco style NYC '80s groove... Loaded with claustrophobic

nervous tension, these tracks make use of taut, minimalistic,

hypnotically repetitive rhythms that make us imagine an Electro

version of AQ faves Circle. The atmosphere is further enhanced by the

inclusion of sound FX from the film and snippets of dialogue - the

tough guy banter of Kurt Russell's wiseass anti-hero "Snake" Plissken

is priceless, especially due to Russell's constipated Clint Eastwood

delivery.

Set in a dystopian, wartorn then-future of 1997, Escape From New

York sends "Snake" into the maximum security prison that the island of

Manhattan has become to rescue (at pain of death) the President of the

United States, who is being held captive there by the city's "inmates"

after Air Force One made a particularly poor choice of a place to

crash-land! The film also stars the likes of Lee Van Cleef, Ernest

Borgnine, Harry Dean Stanton, Adrienne Barbeau, Donald Pleasence, and

Isaac Hayes, all of 'em capable of chewing the scenery right along

with Russell. You definitely don't need to have seen Escape From New

York to get a kick out of this soundtrack, but the movie is an

enjoyable '80s action thriller worthy of its cult status and listening

to this will probably get you to go rent it.

Carpenter composed and performed the score in association with

sound designer Alan Howarth, whose home studio (stocked with an ARP

Quadra, an ARP Avatar with 16 step sequencer, a Prophet 5 programmable

analog synthesizer, and a Linn LM-1 drum machine) was used for the

recording. Howarth was further responsible for remixing and

remastering the album for the release of this "expanded edition" which

first appeared in 2000. It includes several cues originally meant for

scenes deleted from the theatrical release of the film, and runs to

over 57 minutes in length. 'Bout time it was back in circulation!

This vinyl version includes a poster of its exclusive over art,

by Jay 'Iron Jaiden' Shaw, along with liner notes from both Howarth

and Shaw.

HOUSE BY THE CEMETERY (WALTER RIZZATI) "OST" (Death Waltz) lp 29.00

Another fantastic new release from our favorite horror movie

soundtrack reissue label, Death Waltz, this one the creepy psychedelic

soundtrack to Lucio Fulci's 1981 supernatural splatterfest House By

The Cemetery, this time not scored by his longtime musical partner

Fabio Frizzi, but instead, a fella by the name of Walter Rizzati, who

previously was maybe best known for a handful of sexploitation

comedies, but he adapts pretty darn well, delivering a very Frizzi

like collection of dark swirling psychedelia, and organ heavy,

phantasmic, almost Goblin-ish Italo-prog moodiness.

It's been ages since we've seen House By The Cemetery, so the

plot eludes us, it's a twisty tangly one like all Fulci movies, we do

know there's a mysterious dead girl, a murdered woman, a strange

locked cellar, and a Victorian surgeon who has been experimenting on

humans to become immortal? And is hundred of years old? The liner

notes say it's one of Fulci's more coherent plots, but fear not, it's

still batshit insane! And totally worth seeing, as are all the movies

Death Waltz tackles, besides rescuing amazing lost soundtracks, DW's

reissue campaign is a great classic horror primer as well!

Sonically, House By The Cemetery fits pretty well with past

Frizzi / Fulci collaborations, Rizzati unfurling lilting melodies over

darkly drifting bog-fog shimmer, heartfelt synths wreathed in reverb

and echo, peppered with little piano flourishes, those gentle melodic

moments balanced by tense and intense haunted house church organ

fugues, as well as some darkly atmospheric psych prog. Creepy voices

drift wraithlike over buzzing synths and twisted FX, swirling

atmospheric proggy ambience gives way to almost symphonic swells,

there's some heavy psych guitar too, flanged and freaky, but none of

this is actual 'psychedelic rock' like some other scores we've dug

recently, the guitars are woven into a much broader psychedelic

sprawl. Although some tracks, like "Blonk Monster" (named for Izzati's

co-composer Alessandro Blonksteiner) do have a seriously fuzzy psych

guitar main riff, the song sounding like it could burst into hard rock

at any moment, but instead, it splinters into dense swirling clouds of

hazy psychedelic noise, cymbal shimmer and tranced out drones, before

returning to that doomy riffy prog-psych creep. It's definitely the

track we find ourselves returning to, but there are plenty more

moments like that throughout. Needless to say, super recommended, and

it definitely has us wanting to see the movie again too...

Pressed on blood red vinyl, housed in a gorgeous jacket with

stunning new artwork by artist Graham Humphreys, whose new art is

definitely in keeping with the spirit of classic Italian horror movie

posters. On the back of the jacket are some killer shots from the

movies as well as extensive liner notes on the back cover, about the

score and the film. Inside is a 12" x 12" print of the cover art, as

well as a massive fold out poster version as well. And more notes on

the printed inner sleeve from Humphreys about his inspiration for the

artwork.

LAST HOUSE ON THE LEFT (DAVID HESS) "OST" (One Way Static) lp 29.00

First there was Death Waltz, then Waxwork, and now One Way

Static throws their bloodied and battered hat into the horror

soundtrack vinyl reissue ring, with their reissue of David Hess's

soundtrack to Wes Craven's depraved exploitation classic Last House On

The Left. We only just got these in, so we'll likely give this a more

complete write up down the line, but wanted to include it on this

week's special horror soundtrack vinyl in-between list.

David Hess not only composed the music, but was also one of the

actors in the movie as well! His songs are a strangely lovely contrast

to the horrific images on screen, a legendarily stomach churning

display of depravity and ultraviolence, much of it perpetrated by Hess

himself!!

The packaging is super swank as well, deluxe tip-on style

gatefold jackets, with printed inner sleeves, no doubt Death Waltz

fans (and Waxwork fans too) will dig big time! Note, this is also

eventually coming out on as a limited edition digipack cd, with 4

bonus tracks. Both formats include extensive liner notes and photos.

LET THE RIGHT ONE IN (JOHAN SODERQVIST) "OST" (Death Waltz) lp 29.00

Another excellent installment in the ongoing series of classic

horror score vinyl reissues from UK label Death Waltz. So far we've

seen some of our favorite soundtracks get the deluxe reissue

treatment: Escape From New York, Halloween II, Halloween III, Zombi 2,

Prince Of Darkness, heavy on the John Carpenter for sure, even some

soundtracks we hadn't heard of before like Living Dead At The

Manchester Morgue. And now we have Let The Right One In, easily one of

the best modern vampire movies, and notable in the Death Waltz catalog

for being the first modern score (with more apparently on the way),

and even beyond that, it's a strange fit sonically as well, as Johan

Soderqvist's score is much more of a traditional, lush symphonic big

budget Hollywood movie score, but it's still a good one, and for folks

who've seen the film, the cues here will immediately evoke some iconic

images from the film. But even if you haven't seen the movie, the

music here is quite beautiful, and stirring, soaring swoonsome

strings, minor key melodies, darkly emotional, haunting and

melancholic, long passages of brooding and mesmerizing minimalism,

with tracks like "The Slaughter" and "Hiding The Body", as the titles

and the corresponding scenes in the film might suggest, unfurling as

much darker, atonal affairs, adding dissonance, and creating tense /

intense dronescapes, laced with subtle percussion, clouds of cymbal

shimmer, moaning brass, delicate crystalline pianos, all set amidst

softly roiling billows of low end thrum, sounding more like something

you might here on Type or Miasmah. And like some of our other favorite

scores, there are recurring melodic motifs, and Soderqvist deftly

creates the sort of gorgeous haunting melodic themes that get lodged

in your head, and become forever linked with the mood and emotions of

the film.

There are a few tracks that border on the maudlin, it is after

all a big time symphonic score, and to be fair those moments make

perfect sense in the context of the film, but the majority of the

score is a much darker, and more ominously brooding affair, which does

make it in the end, a pretty excellent match over all for the Death

Waltz series.

And like the rest of the Death Waltz lps, the record comes

housed in that super distinctive black and blue bordered Death Waltz

sleeve, with a new original painting, depicting the main character,

sitting alone, in the snow, on the jungle gym in front of a

dilapitated apartment complex, beneath a black sky. Includes a printed

inner sleeve, liner notes by both Soderqvist and cover artist Candice

Tripp, as well as a large A2 sized poster of the cover art.

LIVING DEAD AT THE MANCHESTER MORGUE (GIULIUNO SORGINI) "OST" (Death

Waltz) lp 29.00

Death Waltz is fast becoming one of our favorite reissue labels,

all vinyl, all horror or suspense movie soundtracks, many of them

extremely obscure, some not so much, all lavishly packaged in deluxe

jackets with all new artwork, and extensive liner notes. It's almost

impossible to resist the urge to collect them all, which is proving to

be pretty difficult, considering how many records they're releasing,

and the fact that they're a little pricey, but they're worth it, if

you've seen/heard any of these, you know it's true. And while they've

all been pretty stellar, this one might just be one of our faves.

The Living Dead At The Manchester Morgue, aka Let Sleeping

Corpses Lie, is a rad seventies hippie zombie movie about a serious of

Manson like murders which are blamed on a hippie couple, when in fact

the grisly crimes were committed by a horde of zombies brought back

from the dead by a machine that uses radiation to kill insects!!

It's pretty crazy, and while it's been years since we've seen

it, we remember being pretty blown away. But what we didn't remember,

was how goddamn good, and crazy weird, the soundtrack was. A dizzying

array of symphonic arrangements, funky almost disco-y grooves (with

some breakbeats to DIE for), creepy operatic vocals, haunting

dronescapes, and all manner of crazy 'zombie' vocals. Even separated

from the film, the music here remains creepy and cool, fluttering

woodwinds drift over a chorus of moans and groans, weird vocalizations

meant to be zombies, countered by soaring plaintive female vox,

crooning creepy laments, that at once point devolve into maniacal

laughter. One of the tracks here is a wicked organ drive groove, heavy

on the percussion, with a wild drum solo, the sort of thing we can't

imagine hasn't been sampled like crazy. Strings soar, chaotic

percussion all over the place, long layered drones, sinister black

ambience, and the whole record is peppered with those strange undead

vocal growlings and mewlings, the director of the film even

contributes some vocals, doing his best crazed zombie. Some of the

tracks even have a Perrey & Kingsley vibe, sorta playful and melodic,

but the score is never far from another stretch if blackened sinister

cinematic ambience. We've been listening to this like crazy, and it's

making us want to see the movie again too!

As we mentioned before, super swank packaging, that immediately

recognizable Death Waltz blue and black sleeve, with super striking

painting on the cover. Notes from the composer on the back of the

jacket, the inside sleeve featuring extensive liner notes AND a

beautiful poster of the cover art.

PRINCE OF DARKNESS (JOHN CARPENTER & ALAN HOWARTH) "OST" (Death

Waltz) lp 29.00

Death Waltz does it again. This amazing new soundtrack vinyl

reissue label resurrects another John Carpenter classic, 1987's Prince

Of Darkness, a trippy horror movie about the discover of an 'anti-god'

in a strange vial of green liquid, there's messages sent through time,

tachyons, MATH. It's a doozy, and the soundtrack is pretty much

perfect. Carpenter once again teams up with Alan Howarth, the two

having collaborated on the soundtracks for Halloween II and III,

Escape From New York, They Live and loads more, and the sounds here

will be immediately familiar to fans of those other soundtracks, even

if they've never seen Prince Of Darkness. The opening is classic

Carpenter/Howarth, a whipping wind beneath a dark sinister bassline,

just a handful of notes, sort of like the beginning of an eighties

rock song, but instead of breaking into a song proper, those notes

just loop ominously, over and over and over, underpinning a haunting

six note refrain, the sort of thing Carpenter and Howarth are masters

of crafting, evocative and haunting, an earworm on par with the

Halloween theme. Other abstract sounds swirl around the theme, muted

pulses, distant drones, haunting choir like vocals. And the

interesting thing about this soundtrack, moreso than any of

Carpenter's others, is that all of the tracks here are essentially

variations of that single theme, that bassline, and that melody,

resurfacing in multiple incarnations, some lush and almost symphonic,

others spare and nearly silent, hushed and harrowing, and still

others, pounding and sinister and a little bit new wave sounding.

The majority of the second side is taken up by "The Devil

Awakens", which stretches the preceding sounds way out, into a grim,

sprawl of spectral drift, lots of drifting drones and abstract

ambience, punctuated with strangely out of place horns, mysterious

voices, that melody hidden just beneath the surface, very tense, and

darkly ominous. So great!

And as with all Death Waltz stuff, the packaging is fantastic,

that immediately recognizable red black and blue cover, with a super

striking new painting, a strange figure with beams of light shooting

into a black sky, the title in a pool of blood red, multiple eclipses,

and some scary figures lurking in the background, that same painting

is reproduced on the big poster included inside. The back of the

jacket features liner notes from Howarth, while the inside sleeve

features notes on the cover painting from artist Sam Smith.

RE-ANIMATOR (RICHARD BAND) "OST" (Waxwork) lp 29.00

The crazy success of UK horror movie soundtrack vinyl reissue

label Death Waltz seems to have inspired someone else to start a

similar concern, so similar in fact, that we were initially convinced

that this new imprint Waxwork, MUST be some sort of Death Waltz sub

label. Besides being a horror movie soundtrack vinyl reissue label,

Waxwork also gets modern artists to design new artwork for the cover,

they include a printed inner sleeve with notes from the composer and

the director, they also include a poster of the new artwork, the cover

is even adorned with a sticker that looks EXACTLY like a Death Waltz

sticker, right down to the font and the layout. So yeah, no points for

originality, but who can argue with a vinyl reissue of the score to

Stuart Gordon's infamous/legendary/genius 1985 horror comedy classic

Re-Animator, which if you haven't seen it, you should. Right now. It's

gross, and ridiculous, and totally over the top, and scary and really

funny, probably actor Jeffrey Combs' finest moment, and there is of

course the infamous head-giving-head scene (so infamous it also gets

referenced in the liner notes). It's definitely a fine choice for a

first release, and not just because the movie is great, the score too,

by Richard Band is really excellent. Heavily beholden to Bernard

Hermann, and supposedly composed as an homage to the classic Hitchcock

thrillers AND Citizen Cane, the liner notes even explain that the

original credits in the film were meant to include the line "with

apologies to Bernard Hermann", which for whatever reason got left out,

and thus ended up with Band often accused of simply ripping off

Hermann. So much so that apparently he once got a letter from the

Hermann estate claiming that if Hermann were still alive, Band would

most definitely have been sued.

And it's a fair cop, the Re-Animator score is a total classic

score, lots of strings, lots of dramatic Psycho like moments, but Band

definitely makes it his own, especially on the instantly recognizable

main theme, which most weirdo movie buffs will recognize instantly.

Tense and intense, dramatic and moody, it's essentially like a Hermann

score with disco drums, which sounds crazy, but it totally works and

sounds amazing. If like us you've seen Re-Animator a million times,

that track will instantly bring back a flood of images, crazy, eye

popping, gut busting blood soaked images, which is what the best

scores SHOULD do. Nothing else on the score is as cool and crazy as

the disco drummed theme, but it's all pretty great, and even removed

from the film, still makes for a fantastic listen.

And as if we even need to describe it, this new version comes in

a heavy sleeve, with cool creepy very GREEN cover art by artist Gary

Pullin, inside there's a printed inner sleeve with rare photos and

liner notes from both director Stuart Gordon and composer Richard

Band, there's also a huge poster of Pullin's new art, and the record

itself is pressed on heavy 180 gram green and glow in the dark swirled

vinyl!

THE DEVIL'S BUSINESS (JUSTIN GREAVES) "OST" (Death Waltz) lp 28.00

Another awesome new release from UK vinyl horror movie

soundtrack reissue label Death Waltz, the soundtrack to 2011's The

Devil's Business, which we have yet to see, but the trailer looks

pretty incredible. While Death Waltz does generally tend to focus on

vintage horror soundtracks, this is not their first foray into modern

soundtracks, having recently also released the Let The Right One In.

The man behind the music for the The Devil's Business is Justin

Greaves, who many aQ-ers should know as a member ofof sludgelords Iron

Monkey, as well as the mainman in atmospheric post rock brooders

Crippled Black Phoenix, which makes sense, as the music here is not

that far removed from Crippled Black Phoenix. The excellently titled

opener "My Enemies I Fear Not, But Protect Me From My Friends",

perfectly lays out the sonic template sounding like a haunting,

brooding, slow burn post rock, loping and doomy, soaring and darkly

majestic, the vocals though a deep croon, the sort of voice to imagine

singing old hymns in Latin, which perfectly fits the vibe. There's

some Morricone-ish twang too, and swirling strings, tense and moody,

building to a seriously intense climax, before slipping back into a

droney brood.

And the thing is, unlike a lot of scores and soundtracks, only a

handful of these tracks are cues, most are proper songs, hard to say

if the whole songs are used in the movie or just parts, but those

tracks are pretty great, droney, and dark, psychedelic and smoldering,

super atmospheric and cinematic (obviously), laced with synths and

strings, buzzing guitars, plodding rhythms, a few tracks have a gypsy

vibe to them, sounding almost circusy, others are slow, creepy doom

folk drifts, occasionally laced with operatic vocals, or field

recordings, but just as often, allowed to creep ominously, and some

songs full on rock sounding like Godspeed or Explosions In The Sky or

yeah, Crippled Black Phoenix.

A few tracks get reprised at the end of the record, one song is

revisited, only spookier (the 'spooky version'), another is an

alternate version that recasts the original as sounding more like

Goblin or John Carpenter, and one of the shorter jams gets fleshed out

as a huge, majestic post rock blow out. The shorter cues are pretty

great too, from ghostly high end ambient shimmer, to woozy, sinister

synthscapery.

As with all Death Waltz, the packaging is super swank, the lp

has a killer spot varnished cover, with new art, that makes the movie

seem like it could be from the seventies, a huge poster inside as well

as liner notes from Greaves. So cool!!

TWINS OF EVIL (HARRY ROBINSON) "OST" (Death Waltz) lp 29.00

It's hard not to get a bit obsessed with perfectly curated

labels, and few do it as well as UK horror soundtrack reissue label

Death Waltz - every release stunningly packaged, with super

interesting liner notes, amazing new artwork from incredible artists,

and the soundtracks themselves, running the gamut from must haves like

all the classic Carpenter soundtracks (They Live, Halloween III,

Escape From New York), to super obscurities (Living Dead At Manchester

Morgue) to modern soundtracks (The Devil's Business, Let The Right One

In). We find ourselves anxiously awaiting every new Death Waltz

release, whether we've seen the movie or not, heck, whether we've even

heard of the movie at all. We have actually heard of Twins Of Evil,

from 1971, set in nineteenth century Europe and starring Peter Cushing

as a witch/vampire hunter, who is tasked with caring for orphaned twin

girls, who may or may not be EVIL! A classic Hammer Horror for sure,

produced right when the age restriction for British films was relaxed

a bit, which means like many movies of the time, Twins Of Evil is more

violent and sexually explicit than many Hammer films. We have yet to

see the movie, but the stills look pretty amazing, Cushing as the

dapper, troubled witchhunter, a gaggle of scantily clad, nubile

vampire witches, what's not to like!

The music itself was recorded by Harry Robinson, who had written

songs for the Eurovision song contest, the Children's Film Foundation

and even Nick Drake! He had some pop chart hits, but for Twins Of

Evil, not his only Hammer horror score incidentally, he looked to the

spaghetti westerns for inspiration, and it's easy to hear in

Robinson's compositions, with some of the tracks sounding like they

could have been plucked from some obscure Morricone score (sans twang

and whistling of course), the score is bombastic and dramatic,

swirling strings, martial snares, booming timpani's, those strings are

sawed ominously and almost violently in places, leading to stretches

of the soundtrack that dip into a sound more traditionally sinister,

with more obvious horror score moments, ominous drones, tense minor

key strings, a good balance for the more orchestral movements. Again,

it's hard to say how the score matches up to the visuals, but on their

own, it's pretty fantastic.

And like all Death Waltz releases, totally deluxe and over the

top packaging, the lp pressed on red vinyl, housed in a printed

sleeve, with notes from artist Eelus, who was responsible for the

stunning new cover art, and who jokes that he thought he was being

asked to design a poster for Twins, with Danny Devito and Arnold

Schwarzenegger, and goes on to profess his undying love for horror

movies. That's housed in a gorgeous spot varnish heavy jacket, with

film specific liner notes on the back, as well as stills from the

film. Also included a 12"x12" print of the cover art as well as a huge

fold out poster.

ZOMBI 2 (FABIO FRIZZI) "OST" (Death Waltz) lp 29.00

We've been flipping out over pretty much all of the amazing

Death Waltz soundtrack reissues, but for whatever reason, one of the

very first ones they did somehow we never listed, 'cause it was so

limited we never had enough, but they repressed it at some point, so

here it is, and we're so happy, cuz it's a doozy, the movie itself

gory and fantastically grotesque, originally released in 1979, and

directed by none other than Lucio Fulci, his most famous movie by far,

and the one that established him as a master of horror. The soundtrack

by Fabio Frizzi is the perfect accompaniment, equal parts trancey

Italo synth grooviness, progged out Mellotron psychedelia, coked out

Euro-disco, tribal tropical weirdness, shimmering electronics and

moody minimal mesmer, all woven into a strangely compelling sonic

horror all its own.

As with all Death Waltz releases, the packaging is exquisite,

eye popping new cover art, with printed inner sleeves, includes liner

notes from Frizzi himself as well as renowned Italian horror expert

Stephen Thrower. And according to the label, the record is "mastered

at 45 rpm of maximum stereo destruction."
