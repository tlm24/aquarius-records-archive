Beloved Customers and Friends:

Ta da, at last, for this week's in-between 'list', we present the aQ customer "Top Tens" of 2015 compilation!

Yup, it's March already, definitely about time we posted all the top tens of 2015 lists that you fanatics sent our way!! 2016 has had some great releases already, sure, but there was lots of fantastic stuff last year yet to be heard by some of us, so let's take a look back.

As always, AMAZING lists, folks. We'll be digging through 'em for sure looking for gems we missed, you should too.. such diversity and depth, impressive, again, as always. Gotta say, our customers sure know their stuff! There were a few titles that got repeat mentions from various folks, for sure, but also tons of stuff mentioned by, like, only one person, which is exciting too - you guys listen to a LOT of music.

And thanks for all the kind words that accompanied your lists, we really appreciate it - stuff like David & Jade writing, "Thank you for continuing to open our minds with great new music!" or Sean saying: "aQ ruuuuuuuuuuuuuuuuules!", that is always nice to hear. Thanks! You all rule too.

Oh, and speaking of ruling, the lucky list-maker who won this year's 'random list gets a $25 aQ gift certificate' contest was... drum roll... SEAN SLEIGHT of Seattle. Congrats!

All right, read on and take some notes. We are. Definitely being reminded of some stuff we still need to check out.

Ok, here are all your 2015 aQ customer faves, in no particular order (hearing that a lot). It's posted on our website permanently at http://www.aquariusrecords.org/2015customerfaves.html as well, as are our aQ staff top tens of 2015, if you didn't already peruse them, they can be found here: http://www.aquariusrecords.org/stafffaves2015.html

But, before you take a look at everybody's lists, two other things while we have your attention.

Firstly - long time readers of the aQ list are likely familiar with our pal Aaron Aites. Besides being the mastermind behind weirdo noise-pop combo Iran (who released two killer records on Andee's tUMULt label), he also made (along with his partner Audrey Ewell) Until The Light Takes Us, a killer documentary about Norwegian black metal.

Besides all that, he's a super nice guy, and has long been a good friend, a big part of our lives, as well as a big part of aQuarius.

Aaron was recently diagnosed with an extremely aggressive and difficult to treat form of cancer, and as is sadly so often the case, like many musicians and filmmakers, he does not have the sort of insurance that will pay for the experimental and aggressive therapies required to battle this kind of cancer and to survive.

So please if you can, donate a little bit or a lot. If you loved Iran, or Until The Light Takes Us, or just wanna help out someone truly special in their time of need, every little bit helps.

https://www.gofundme.com/e3pbfwrj

JACOB (Acephale Winter, Dunnock, & Co.)

This list is a little bit late because I got overwhelmed trying to narrow my favorite albums of the year to list of merely ten. Finally I just completely abandoned the effort all together and decided to give you a list dedicated to one sub-genre (I can tell you that CEKE, Prurient, and Apprentice Destroyer were finalists though). Anyway, here's my picks for best TECHNOISE albums of 2015. I've also included short blurbs below, in case anyone's interested, and a Spotify playlist, because of course there's a playlist.

Best TECHNOISE of 2015 (in alphabetical order)

-Blood Music: Chicks/Badgering

-Container: LP

-ENA: Divided

-Helm: Olympic Mess

-Kerridge: Always Offended Never Ashamed

-M.E.S.H.: Piteous Gate

-The Prostitutes: Truncheon Cadence (Parts 1 & 2)

-Rabit: Communion

-Ron Morelli: A Gathering Together

-Russell Haswell: As Sure as Night Follows Day

https://player.spotify.com/user/bishoparius/playlist/193R6SmLGsitTlcuxYNSLZ

1. Blood Music - Chicks/Badgering: Just four short tracks, but what it lacks in run time this EP makes up for with an aggressive combination of clattering live percussion, atonal post-post-punk guitar squalls, and pounding sheets of static.

2. Container - LP: Every Container full length is titled "LP" and every one is a banger, but the latest ratchets the energy level up to almost gabber levels. At the same time the production is significantly cleaner and almost conventional, at least until the given track climaxes and is smothered by harsh squeals of Power Electronics inspired feedback,

3. ENA - Divided: Ostensibly Japanese producer ENA makes Drum & Bass, but stripped of everything except Jungle's nervous energy, his tracks are unrecognizable as such. He'd probably object to his music being described as either techno or noise though, even if that's what they most sound like.

4. Helm - Olympic Mess: There's little in the way of beats, per say here, but the influence of dance music is inescapable. Each squeal of feedback is pitch corrected and in tune, each mechanical crank is perfectly quantized. Not a mess at all really.

5. Kerridge - Always Offended Never Ashamed: Kerridge probably did it first, menacing dance floors with distortion saturated beats while other DJs explored dubby minimalism. The world may be starting to catch up, but if Offended is any sign, Kerridge probably still does it best.

6. M.E.S.H. - Piteous Gate: Imagine if a scrap metal noise sculptor like Hal Hutchinson made pulverizing, cantankerous dance tracks. Wouldn't that be awesome? M.E.S.H is awesome.

7. The Prostitutes - Truncheon Cadence (Parts 1 & 2): A double EP of militant, minimalist synth pulses and throbs backed by the barest of bass drum beats. Echoes of both Acid Techno and Chris Carter's Throbbing Gristle era productions abound.

8. Rabit - Communion: "Weightless" is apparently the name of this new genre of post-Dubstep/instrumental Grime (pioneered by Logos and Mumdance), but it's not really an apt descriptor for Communion. Rabit's syncopated scrum of scrapes, clangs, and overdriven gun clocks hits like a ton of scrape metal and feels very weighty indeed.

9. Ron Morelli - A Gathering Together: Nor Morelli runs L.I.E.S, an esteemed bastion of outsider House. His first couple albums for Hospital Productions drew deep from this well and were full of too loud, machine based, sloppy disco grooves. The new is a different beast though, indebted to death industrial. At its funkiest moments it comes across like some tribal dance party in a rusty machine shop. With feedback.

10. Russell Haswell - As Sure as Night Follows Day: Russell Haswell makes harsh noise. Or at least he did until Oscar Powell convinced him to make some "dance music" for his oddball Diagonal label (home to Blood Music and Consumer Electronics, of all projects). The results are still harsh, and often noisy, but instead of making music with effects pedals and microphones Haswell's building it from the debris of rave culture. You can't really dance to it, but I doubt that's the point.
