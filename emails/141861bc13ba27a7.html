ASH BORER "Bloodlands" (Gilead Media) lp 16.98

Latest blast of tranced out black buzz from this Arcata horde,

and quite possibly their best, which is saying a lot considering how

much we've dug pretty much every goddamn thing they've released. We

were actually lucky enough very recently to see these guys play in the

middle of the night, in the middle of the forest, barely lit on a

stage in the middle of nowhere, beneath millions of stars, and it was

seriously transcendent, their songs less 'songs' and more sort of

movements, organic fields of dense droned out sound, totally

mesmerizing and utterly hypnotic, the sort of buzzing blackness that

simultaneously inspires frenzied headbanging AND lulls you into a

glorious torpor. We were sprawled out on a big rock right in front of

the stage, and the sound was overwhelming, we were totally entranced,

and that vibe is perfectly reproduced here. the band infusing their

raw black buzz with more melody then ever, but without losing any of

the furious blackened energy that made their music so compelling. Two

side long tracks, the first, beginning all washed out and shimmery,

before exploding into a crushing avalanche of sound, one that's super

psychedelic and weirdly produced, with at least one passage that

sounds like the thundering of horses' hooves orchestrated into dense

squalls of buzzing black noise. And of course for all the tranced out

stretches of blurry buzz, there are sprawls of lumbering doomic churn,

of roiling tribal drum driven drones, even some Caretaker-ish drift,

all gauzy and woozy and hauntingly lovely.

The B side too, begins with a long intro, of super melodic,

minor key guitar, slow smoldering riffage, over chiming tones, and

strange industrial rumbles and pulsations, and this time, instead of

exploding into buzzing blackness, the sound blossoms into a loping

doom, all spidery distorted guitars, and pounding double kick

drumming, although that moody doom does eventually burst into more

furious blackness, a churning heaviness that seems to somehow be both

doom and black metal at the same time. more midtempo than blast, but

the energy is pure grim blackness, laced with soaring melodies, and

punctuated by super dynamic arrangements, and unexpected textural and

compositional shifts. Some darkly emotional, seriously next level

blackness, that while perhaps best enjoyed under the stars in the

middle of a deep dark forest, sounds pretty goddamn good right here.

On black vinyl, limited to 800 copies, includes an insert and a

download code.

ASTRAL SOCIAL CLUB "Electric Yep" (Trensmat) lp 21.00

Another new release on the Trensmat label. Last week we

highlighted the latest from Italian cosmic psych folk siblings My Cat

Is An Alien, and now there's this, the latest from Neil Campbell,

formerly of Vibracathedral Orchestra, who now creates distorto

electronica as Astral Social Club, having seemingly ditched the ur-

drone ragas of his former outfit for something more fractured and beat

driven - although you'd be forgiven for thinking this was in fact VCO,

based on the first track. Sure there are beats, but they're spare and

abstract, and mostly seem to be adding rhythmic texture, to a shimmery

field of folky almost Appalachian sounding guitar strum, and swirling,

smoldering buzz and thrum. Hazy, and dreamy, and druggy, a glorious

washed out drift that's super mesmerizing and ultra hypnotic, and

goddamn if we wouldn't have been perfectly happy if this track had

filled up both side of this here lp. But this is Astral Social Club,

so the second track explodes into an FX heavy frenzy of pounding

almost happy hardcore sounding beats, buried beneath an avalanche of

swirling psychedelic dream-noise, wild tangles of blown out shred, a

swirling, dizzying sonic squall, that sans those beats might sound

more like the more abstract moments of Birchville Cat Motel or Our

Love Will Destroy The World, albeit with some heavy psych overtones.

And like past ASC jams, it's pretty irresistible, in some ways, it's

almost like some warped hybrid of caveman techno and Japanese psych,

which for most aQ-ers we would imagine would be pretty goddamn enticing.

The rest of the record seems to split the difference between the

more dense, driving, beat heavy noisescapey, and more washed out,

ethereal kosmische sprawl, "Rainbow Weather" sounding almost like

Astral Social Club new age, again, still slathered in buzz and thrum

and wreathed in a halo of squiggly effects. But then again, "Twenty-

Four Times Yes" swings back into some alien electronica, the beats

buried beneath another wild concoction of druggy swirl, fractured

noise and playful primitive electronics. "Valley Of The Left-Handed

Depilator" seems to emerge full formed from the pervious track, but

quickly blissed out into a soaring shimmering dream-drift epic, the

churning rhythm, not so much adding propulsion, as just more texture,

totally heady and super psychedelic, before things finally finish off

with "Boiling Up The Bones", which sounds like Astral Social Club via

Kompakt, minimal and spare, but with some weirdo sounds, tweaked

percussion, and plenty of twisted shit going on below the surface.

As always, crazy limited, already sold out at the label, we may

be able to get more, but probably best to snag one now just in case.

And includes a link to a full download of the record as well.

AWAY "Cities" (Utech) cd 14.98

Could this be any cooler of a concept for aQuarius consumption?!

It's a disc of urban field recordings, it's on the always-excellent

experimental Utech label, and it's by none other than Michel "Away"

Langevin, best known as the amazing drummer and visual art director of

French-Canadian sci-fi prog-thrash metal legends VOIVOD!! Heck, we

love Voivod, we love Utech, and we love found sounds and environmental

recordings. And this is a good one. Away, who did the cool cover art

in his distinctive Voivodian style, has apparently been keeping his

ears active outside of playing in his band, seeking out and recording

interesting sounds - from the natural to the man-made, and often

musical - while wandering about his hometown of Montreal or wherever

in the world Voivod's touring has taken him. As per the album title,

the pieces on this disc were each recorded in different cities -

Montreal, Chicago, Mexico City, and various burgs in Europe. Edited

into concentrated sound-collages, each track here holds many unique

charms, rapidly unfolding in seamlessly segueing ambient soundbites,

this sorta "plunderphonic" approach to real world recordings getting

somewhat dizzying in its kaledoscopic changes from moment to moment.

You'll hear the droning hum of passing traffic, industrial noises,

church bells chiming, loudspeaker announcements, birds twittering,

crowds chanting (sports fans? protesters?), what sounds like an ethnic

festival or three, and lots of MUSIC, snippets of many sorts - it's

part of our environment more than we know, as this disc proves. Away's

microphone was drawn to all kinds of busking street musicians,

marching bands, radios playing in stores, boom boxes blaringâ€¦ there's

pop and classical and gypsy folk and more.

These recordings sound celebratory at times, intimate at others,

but always convey a sheer love of sound. They definitely transport the

listener, as effective field recordings do, right into very specific

and "real" yet still somehow mysterious places and times. Anyone who

likes the methodology of many of the Sublime Frequencies releases that

take us armchair travellers to other lands will enjoy delving into this.

We've read that Away has said, "I've always been fascinated by

field recordings, tape collage and musique concrete. I was introduced

to it by [late Voivod guitarist] Piggy as a youngster." Still, who

would have thought that Away's first solo album would be a disc of

field recordings?

It's interesting, since Voivod tends to imaginationally explore

the outer reaches of the cosmos in their space-metal constructs, that

for this solo project Away would come up with something so "down to

earth", but the spirit of exploration is the same.

As usual with Utech, a nicely designed package, comes in slim

but heavy black jacket printed with Away's art in metallic silver and

white ink. Limited to 500 copies.

BLOUSE "Imperium" (Captured Tracks) cd/lp 14.98/19.98

Full length number two from these new wave / chill wave retro

revivalists, which surprisingly finds the duo ditching their synths

and drum machines completely, and crafting a gorgeous blast of guitar

driven, nineties style indie rock dream pop that we're digging like

crazy. Just give a listen to the title track, all processed ethereal

female vox, draped over acoustic guitar strum, and some shimmery

swirls of shoegaze guitar buzz, simple drumming, all anchored by some

murky Joy Division-y bass, a hazy swirl of killer hooks, warm

psychedelic jangle, a little guitar rock crunch, all sculpted into

some seriously catchy gloom poppy dreaminess, that positions Blouse

Mk. II closer to groups like the Dum Dum Girls, or Beach House, their

sound woozy and druggy, seemingly channeling the spirt of the Velvets

and Mazzy Star, through something a bit fuzzier and janglier. Our

favorite tracks tend to be the crunchier, fuzzier, noisier ones (check

out "In A Glass"), but even with some sonic heft and distorto guitar

buzz, the sound remains gloriously washed out, dreamy, druggy and

drifty. LP includes download too...

BODY/HEAD "Coming Apart" (Matador) cd/lp 14.98/26.00

With the breakup of Sonic Youth, we have Kim Gordon's vision and

voice coming through her new project Body/Head, a duo with Bill Nace

-- a New England improviser whose run of collaborators also includes

Michael Morley, Thurston Moore, Chris Corsano, etc. Nace's freeform

strategery of low-impact, buzzsaw guitar blur met with stompbox

feedback-dinosaur roar dynamics is equalled by Kim Gordon's

intergenerational channelling of Patty Waters and Jandek, in separate

but equal parts. The songs are decidedly atonal, zombified, anti-blues

numbers, following what Gordon brought to the experimental portion of

the Sonic Youth equation, especially, her songs from those obtuse SYR

albums. Anybody hoping for "Kool Thing" should know better, as she's

long passed out of her pop-tune phase. Small two note phrases waft in

and out of the minor-chord slumping strum, occasionally punctuated by

squallid scratchiness, purposefully wonked cable hum, and "I just shit

my pants" bursts of guitar excitement.

HEBOSAGIL "Lahto" (Ektro) cd/lp 14.98/22.00

It was really only a matter of time before Finnish stoner-sludge

noise rock weirdos Hebosagil found their way to Jussi from Circle's

super eclectic and equally weirdo Ektro label, we're sort of surprised

it took this long actually. The metalheads around aQ have long been

fans of Hebosagil, whose sound in the past touched on lots of our slo-

mo sludge favorites (Sleep, Bunkur, the Body, Monarch, etc), but who

here on their most recent, ditch much of that glacial tarpit crawl for

something a bit more high energy, a punked out metal that borrows from

classic old school heavy metal as much as modern day pigfuck noise

ruck crush, the songs almost proggy in their arrangement, slipping

easily from blasting almost blackened blasts, to woozy stonery sludge,

to wild noisy churn, to swaggery, almost Stooges-y hard rocking, the

most distinctive thing the vocals, which sound really Finnish to us,

if that makes any sense, it's not hard to imagine Hebosagil as some

twisted Jussi side project, super dramatic and way up in the mix, a

raspy gravelly bellow that sounds more like Turbonegro or Scissorfight

or Fucked Up than any vokills that might be more appropriate, but it's

that strange mix, that makes Lahto so appealing. And while the band

may be more rocking these days, they can still lay down some seriously

sludgy downtuned heaviness, the band constantly locking into churning

caveman riffage and neanderthal drum plod, but then those vocals come

in, and it's transformed into something much more strange, and really,

much more fun. Not to say this is a 'fun' record, it's still plenty

heavy and dark, noisy and chaotic and metallic, but it's also Finnish,

which means it's geniusly fucked, totally batshit rocking weirdness

that you can add to your Finnish whatthefuck collection, which by now

includes (or really SHOULD include) Circle, Pharaoh Overlord, Ride For

Revenge, Undor, Stench Of Deacy, Will Over Matter, Arkhamin Kirjasto,

Billy Boys, Death Trip, Dead Reptile Shrine, Steel Mammoth, Uton,

Cardinals Folly, Keuhkot, Lurk and we'd imagine a whole lot more.

MIND OVER MIRRORS "When The Rest Are Up Four" (Immune) cd/lp

14.98/16.98

Harmonium! Tape delay! Oscillator! Oh my, this is ever nice. A

solo project by Peeesseye's Jaime Fennelly, the "harmoniumist", tape

delayer, and oscillator operator in question (he's also responsible

for assorted synth processing, and there's other sounds such as pipe

organ heard on here as well), Mind Over Mirrors is already up to its

fourth album (in only 3 years) with this cd and lp for Immune,

following releases on such labels as Aguirre and Digitalis, that we

foolishly ignored, this being the first Mind Over Mirrors we've

reviewed - and once we tear ourselves away from its warm embrace,

we'll have to look into those earlier outings. 'Cause the six tracks

here, ranging in length from under 4 minutes to over 18, of blissfully

melodic, minimalist repetition, are all quite delightful and droneful

indeed. So soothing and sacred-sounding, some with gently surging

rhythms, others with placid deep drones. The mystic glow emanating

from this release is hard to resist. Our newest nighttime drifting off

to sleep listening favorite (we should make section for that in the

store - come to think of it, we did a whole in-between list about

music like that once, didn't we?), and nice to put on in the morning

when you wake up, too.

Includes some guest guitar from Scott Tuma of Souled American

and Matt Christensen of Zelienople, btw, and also the vinyl version

comes with a download, fyi.

PINKISH BLACK "Razed To The Ground" (Century Media) cd/lp

13.98/16.98

Former aQ Record Of The Weekers Pinkish Black are back with

album number two - surprisingly released by big metal label Century

Media, their first was on Handmade Birds - and it's another lurching

amorphous mass of droning psychedelic deathrock tinged weirdness!

Though they may hail from Texas, Razed to the Ground is an altogether

unearthly album - conjuring mental images of bizarre alien landscapes,

black slime oozing from every pore. It's a sprawling cosmic nightmare,

like H.P. Lovecraft chewing on a mouthful of rotten magic mushrooms.

Pulsing black ambience throbs like leeches slithering up through damp

soil - before erupting in a churn of dizzying cyclical noiserock, all

while science fiction B-movie synths swirl overhead and a ritualistic

voice chants emotionless in the distant void. Pummeling yet patient

drumming recalls the hypnotic grace of latter era Neurosis. The vocals

are an eerie lush croon, at times invoking the same kind of dead-eyed

fanaticism of Peter Murphy or Rozz Williams but while maintaining a

sort of detached ambivalence that actually doesn't sound too far off

from Josh Homme. Bass distortion bubbles like tar while insectoid

synths hum and whine like something from a monstrous Dr. Who

horrorshow. Or maybe a demented zombified Gary Numan. Elsewhere,

creeping haunted house piano sluggishly hammers out a funereal dirge

before tumbling toms usher in an ominous slab of heavy spaced out

synthesized doom rock. Near the album's end, a melody emerges that

could almost be described as uplifting. Almost. But it only serves to

accentuate the dystopian extraterrestrial bleakness of the rest of the

album as the track abruptly fades to silence, before returning with

the lonely haunting pluck of discordant notes and the sound of a chill

biting wind, ending the record in a way that could be described as

about the furthest thing from comforting. But it's certainly

appropriate from a band who named themselves after the grizzly scene

left behind by the suicide of one of their former bandmates. It's not

a pretty journey, but damn is it a fascinating one. If the fringe

synthetic avant-garde heaviness of Wreck & Reference or the decayed

blackened death rock of Atriarch is your curdled cup of tea, then this

is absolute essential listening.

PITRE, DUANE "Bridges" (Important Records) lp + cd 19.98

Newest album by this experimental string composer combining

Eastern tuning with Western compositional structures. Using a variety

of exotic string instruments (cumbus, mandolin and ukelin) with

computer and electronics, Pitre along with a cello and a soprano

saxophone player create two side-long suites that embrace a soundworld

both contemporary and ancient. Similar in feel to the spatial string

composition of his last full-length Feel Free, where the performers

were given only loose instructions to create a more open compositional

approach, this time around Pitre removes that randomizing element and

exerts more compositional control, touching on sonic elements of

accumulation and dissipation for a fluid composition that breathes

with a light touch and a beautifully heavy resonance.

SEBADOH "Defend Yourself" (Joyful Noise) cd/lp 15.98/22.00

At this point, it's probably easier to think of the bands you

used to love, who have NOT reformed, cuz this really seems like the

age of reunions, not that we're complaining... Some of our favorite

bands, alive and kicking, touring and making records again! What's not

to love? And while everyone was freaking out about the resurrection of

Dinosaur Jr, we couldn't help but think, even back when Beyond came

out, Dinosaur's first record in a decade at the time, how amazing it

would be if Lou Barlow reformed Sebadoh. We never stopped being a

little kooky about that band, as much as we loved Dinosaur, Sebadoh

always occupied a super special place in our hearts, in fact both

reissues of The Freed Man and III we made Records Of The Week here,

and heck, we probably should have done the same for Bakesale, so when

Sebadoh actually started playing shows, we were giddy, and when this

new record was announced, holy crap!

So here it is, the first new record in 14 years, since 1999's

The Sebadoh, and we're pleased to report, it really does sort of sound

like it could have come out immediately after The Sebadoh, the sound

is warm and familiar, jangly and fuzzy, dark and broody at times,

goofy and irreverent at others, but Barlow still has a knack for

crafting dark, emotional jangle pop, and his partner in crime Jason

Lowenstein (aka Jake) continues, we can only assume, to add his own

twisted sonic perspective to the proceedings. And like on past

records, there are moments of willful sonic spikiness, and some

sweetly schmaltzy sad boy loner folk wistfulness, and this time some

countryish twang here and there, but most of Defend Yourself, is just

crazy catchy, classic sounding indie rock jangle pop that manages to

channel the spirit and sound of the nineties, into something that

doesn't sound nearly as nostalgic as it could have...

Both the cd and the lp have a swank diecut slipcover-ed packaging.

STARGAZER'S ASSISTANT, THE "Mirrors & Tides, Shivers &

Voids" (Utech) 2x10" 26.00

We've had half of this before, when the aptly titled Shivers &

Voids was released on cd. That 3-track, 25 minute recording is now

paired here on vinyl with a brand new ep from this side project of UK

dark prog acts Guapo and Miasma & The Carousel Of Headless Horses. The

new ep, Mirrors & Tides, brings another assistant into the fold,

special guest Mika Ratto from our Finnish faves, Circle! He sings on

an epic, ominous, abstract 17 minute track entitled "Coral Butterfly".

Keeping with the Finnish theme, they also make use of some interesting

metal percussion instruments collected by the late Finnish avant-garde

jazz drummer Edward Vesala. And like we've previously said about The

Stargazer's Assistant already, the results are creepy, soundtracky,

dramatic droned out weirdness under the stars... stirring and sinister

stuff.

Furthermore, here's what we had to say about Shivers & Voids,

before: It may as well just be one long, programmatic piece, beginning

with the nine minutes or so of "Night Soil", a seductive nightmare

that finds the listener sleepwalking on a ghostly, imaginary

battlefield somewhere, with explosive detonations and snare rolls

violently sundering the heavy-drone-gloom that undergirds the track,

making it even scarier. The title track then provides a 4 and a half

minute interlude of clanking chains, acoustic guitar, and melodic,

monkish chant, heavily layered and effected, delving a bit into

Richard Youngs experimental folk territory, quite nice... And then

"Dream Kingdom", at 11 minutes long this disc's truest epic, also

serves up its most baleful bombast.

Should appeal to fans of the aforementioned acts, as well as the

likes of Kiss The Anus Of A Black Cat, Darsombra, and anything else

you can imagine that would mix field recordings and doomy organ and

electronic noise and droning loops... in other words, right up our

alley, obviously!

Limited to 300 copies. Packaged in a 20" x 20" poster sleeve.

Black vinyl. Includes download.

VHK (GALLOPING CORONERS) "Veled Haraptat Csillagot! (Bite The

Stars!)" (Ektro) cd/lp 14.98/22.00

For those that haven't heard the legendary Hungarian group VHK

(aka Vagtazo Halottkemek, or the Galloping Coroners) before, how the

heck to describe 'em? Well, this primal psychedelic punk ensemble has

been around for years (their first album came out in 1988, but they

had formed as far back as the mid '70s!) and they're for sure a band

equally at home in a radical '80s punk squat, or on an ancient Indo-

European battlefield, or out in space among the stars. As well as at

home now on Ektro / Full Contact, the eccentric label associated with

our fave Finnish space rock weirdos, Circle. They have put out

previous albums on Jello Biafra's Alternative Tentacles label, and can

certainly be considered one of the most significant, and definitely

unique, Eastern European "punk" bands ever. Their sound is part pagan

improv, part raucous gypsy-punk hoedown, part Hawkwind style blast-

off. Their naturally "tribal" sound even reminded one person here, who

hadn't heard 'em before, of Echo And The Bunnymen - but way more raw

and wild and loose and heavy.

They've been on some sort of hiatus for quite a few years now

(their last release came out in 1999), so it was a pleasant and

exciting surprise to find 'em back again with this record, and at full

force! The powerful, expressive voice of VHK's frontman, "Grandpierre

Atilla", leads the charge, singing and screaming with passion in his

native language. Yet, this album begins in a deceptively mellow mood,

VHK conjuring a shamanistic campfire ritual on opening track

"Handshake", at least at first, being acoustic, melodic, and in a way

almost comforting. Those aspects persist, but as the album progresses,

it builds and builds in intensity, always offering much compelling

rhythmic drive and dark atmospheric vibes, culminating in a track,

"Hun Testveriseg" ("The Hun Brotherhood"), recorded live at a festival

last year called Black Noise, where we really can imagine these guys

coming across as reincarnations of their ancestors, channeling their

warrior spirit with electricity and amplification.

You want some "music review math"? Well, there's moments on this

album that sound like the Sun City Girls jamming with Neurosis, and

we'd further suggest that VHK are perfect for anyone who enjoys both

Acid Mothers Temple and Rudimentary Peni.

Of course, if you already know VHK, you'll be happy they're

galloping again! Old fans like us will love this. And for those who

have yet to be trampled underfoot, this is a fantastic introduction to

the mythology of this strange band. We realize that we we've never

previously reviewed anything by VHK (we've only referenced them twice

before on our site, in fact, in reviews of records by countryish space

rockers U.S. Christmas and avant-folky black metallers Hollenthon).

Glad we're able to rectify that now with this write-up.

WINDHAND "Soma" (Relapse) cd/2lp 14.98/25.00

It seems like only yesterday that Virginia's Windhand first

emerged on the scene seemingly from out of nowhere, announcing their

arrival with an enormous earth-splitting debut that left doomheads the

world over cavorting in slow motion ecstasy. But in the year and a

half that's followed their acclaim has only grown (thanks in no small

part to an immense split with Cough) and now their second full length

has arrived with some serious expectations in tow. But never fear!

Because it's another overwhelming stoned monument of blown-out moss-

specked DOOM. The first thing you'll notice about Windhand is just how

damn HEAVY they are, and in the graveyard realm of doom metal that's

no small feat. But just the very tone and weight of Windhand's music

seriously puts most other supposedly "heavy" bands to shame. The

guitars are lumbering funereal paced black magic incantations - all

overdriven and decayed, but unfathomably huge, with the occasional

dreamy affects drenched lead swimming out from under the murkiness.

The sound is so bottom heavy that it's hard to tell where the guitars

end and the bass begins. Instead they simply congeal into one

slithering black leviathan mass, while the drums plod underneath like

slaveship percussion. The vocals seem to be crying out from the other

side of a black hole, ghostly and buried in the mix, sounding

perfectly forlorn and out of reach. They immediately bring to mind

Electric Wizard's landmark Dopethrone, and really we'd be remiss in

not mentioning that Electric Wizard is a pretty obvious touchstone for

this band in general. But there does seem to be a more serious

undercurrent running through Windhand's music. While Electric Wizard's

music is often imbued with a kind of acid biker b-movie kitsch,

Windhand instead trade that in for a sense of urban decay and personal

dread that brings to mind Pallbearer or Strength To Dream-era Warning.

There is very little reprieve from the claustrophobic heaviness. The

only real exceptions are the sparse Americana twang of 'Evergreen's

acoustic folk noir (a song that really allows Dorthia Cottrell's

haunting vocals to shine) and the lonely acoustic minimalism

interspersed into the enormous slow-burn 'Boleskine', which closes out

the album with 30+ minutes of intense heaviness before fading out into

the bleak sounds of howling prairie winds. As a sidenote, we should

also mention that the dudes (& dudette) from the band swung by the

shop when they were in town last week - when we just happened to be

playing the new record in the shop of course! Needless to say they

were super nice and friendly, even going so far as to put us on the

guest list for their show that night! And we're very pleased to report

that when we arrived at the tiny Hemlock Tavern for the performance,

we were subjected to literally one of the loudest, heaviest (and

sweatiest!) live shows we've ever seen, and it really gave us a new

appreciation for the band. Not that we needed another reason to

recommend this record! It goes without saying, but if all things slow

and heavy are what get your motor rumbling, then there's no way you

can pass on this stumbling crumbling testament to relentless heaviness!
