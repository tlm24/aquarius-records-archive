100 FLOWERS "s/t" (Superior Viaduct) lp 15.98

When legendary So-Cal punk rockers the Urinals decided to

retool their sound, and get a little artier, they realized that maybe

the band name the Urinals didn't quite match up with what they were

going for, and thus, the Urinals became 100 Flowers. This is their one

and only full length recording, originally released in 1983, and now

reissued by local label Superior Viaduct. To be fair, the Urinals were

already kinda arty, their sound super minimal, not punk in the sense

of so many punk bands at the time, more sinewy and tribal, the guitars

sharp and jagged, the songs short and spare, and that arty minimalism

was super prescient of much that would follow. And the fact that only

a couple years passed between the Urinals and 100 Flowers, and really,

it's essentially the same band, you might think that that sounds would

be pretty familiar, but in fact, 100 Flowers was a pretty dramatic

shift, the sound much more melodic, less minimal, the sound more full

and more poppy, just check out opener "Without Limbs", total jangle

pop blissout, with crooned vocals, bloopy bass, shimmery guitars, some

kinetic drumming, and a main hook that's pretty tough to resist. But

the band didn't ditch all their angular weirdness, follow up, "All

Sexed Up" is angular, and actually sort of funky, with sung spoken

vocals, and a main guitar part that wouldn't be out of place in a

Minutemen song (a group HEAVILY influenced by the Urinals / 100

Flowers), but even that doesn't sum up the new sound, cuz "Our

Fallout" gets a bit punky, snarly and intense, the vocals venturing

into almost Jello Biafra territory, the guitars fuzzy, the temp

frenetic, but then suddenly, there's the minute long "Horizontal",

with its weird woozy synth solo, and Devo like vocals, and its

stuttering start/stop arrangement.

And they're pretty tough to pin down from there on out, but

essentially, the elements displayed in those first few tracks, make up

the rest of the songs in different measure, the funky punky Minutemen

vibe is present throughout, there's also a surf sound that pops up

constantly, especially in the guitar, and their So-Cal hardcore roots

shine through all over the place, some songs slipping into something

more poppy and jangly, others getting buzzier and punkier, and still

others, slipping into arty angularity, often some twisted mix of the

three.

Catchy and quirky, punky and poppy, this stuff sounds pretty

great even all these years later. And while we always loved the

Urinals, we're now becoming pretty obsessed with 100 Flowers too, and

after having only heard songs on comps here and there, are extremely

grateful to Superior Viaduct for rescuing and reissuing this killer

slab of post punk pop.

Includes a printed insert, and a download code as well.

ALTAR OF PLAGUES "Teethed Glory & Injury" (Profound Lore) cd/2lp

13.98/34.00

The phrase "post black metal" gets bandied around quite a bit

these days. It's seemingly become a catch-all for any BM-leaning band

that doesn't wear corpse paint and/or spikes and doesn't shriek about

Satan and/or Odin, and when there's so much rampant experimentation

going on in a genre it can create a certain desensitization. Nothing's

shocking anymore! That's why it's all the more rare nowadays for a

band to emerge from the black metal scene that could truly be

considered challenging and progressive, but Ireland's Altar of Plagues

are most certainly one of those bands. Teethed Glory and Injury is,

first and foremost, massive. An enormously modern rebuttal to metal's

often backwards-looking tendencies. The album shifts seamlessly from

immersive instrumental drone to frantic blurring blast beats with a

surgical precision. The decayed buzz of the ambient passages (which

make up a pretty big portion of the record) perfectly compliment the

more "metal" tracks without ever feeling like filler. Instead they

feel just as essential, giving the record a sense of patience and an

ebb/flow not often found in this sort of band. The effect is oddly

hypnotic and peaceful, even if at the same time the album is often

undeniably crushing and unnerving. When it isn't lulling you into a

false sense of security with its more spacious moments, it's

absolutely pummeling you with an overwhelming aural assault of hyper

blasts, chilling frigid vokillz and calculated mesmerizing riffs, like

crackling transmissions relayed from an alternate future where the

decomposing urban desolation of early Godflesh and Swans have replaced

the usual Bathory/Darkthrone blueprint as the primary black metal

template. The result is singular and thrilling - a brief and

terrifying glimpse at the ruined dystopic landscape black metal will

one day become. But first we'll have to wait for all of those other

bands to catch up!

DEVO "Hardcore" (Superior Viaduct) 2cd 23.00

Gonna try to keep this relatively short. 'Cause it would be

easy to go on and on and on about what an amazing band Devo were back

in the day and how absolutely ESSENTIAL these newly reissued

collections of the earliest recorded Devo music are. Never before

released on vinyl in the States, the original two volumes of Hardcore

Devo came out on cd and cassette on Rykodisc in 1990 and 1991

respectively, and have been out of print (and sought after by fans)

for many years. Now at last they've been reissued, as separate volumes

on vinyl (one a single lp, the other double) and together as a double

cd set.

Those of you already into Devo, who don't already have this,

NEED this - and even if you do have it, you might want it again,

either to get the vinyl versions, and/or for the 4 previously

unreleased bonus tracks now included (on disc two / Volume Two). For

others, well this is our opportunity to proselytize for the cult of

Devo. Some folks out there who aren't that familiar with Devo might

think of 'em as just some novelty new wave nerd-rock band doing goofy

songs and wearing silly hats, one-hit wonders with 1980's "Whip It!".

And while they did wear silly hats (pardon us, energy domes) and were

plenty goofy, they were also super subversive and bizarre, both

sonically and ideologically, true pioneers in the realm of synth-punk

and new wave, coming up with a sound that somehow grafted Kraftwerk to

punk rock before either were popular, promoting their own

anticapitalist, antiestablishment agenda couched in irony and Dadaism.

These early recordings, demos and rarities recorded on a 4-track in

Akron, Ohio circa 1974-1977, reveal Devo at their most underground,

raw and stripped down, but already possessed of both catchy

songwriting brilliance and conceptual originality. Definitely not the

hippie boogie rock popular at the time!

It's worth noting that both future Devo members Gerald Casale

and Mark Mothersbaugh had been students at Kent State, SDS activists

in fact, and Casale personally witnessed the shocking National Guard

shooting of anti-war demonstrators there in 1970. In some ways, Devo

DRAKE, MOLLY "s/t" (Squirrel Thing) cd/lp 15.98/17.98

Autumn Sunday mornings just got a little sweeter with this

release we have been waiting ages for. We first heard Molly Drake, the

mother of the British folksinger Nick Drake, years ago on a

compilation of early Nick Drake recordings called Family Tree that

featured only a couple of her songs. While that compilation attempted

to position her as a key influence on the popular folksinger, we

weren't quite convinced back then, but we sure are now. Squirrel

Thing, the New York label that brought us the sad and lovely Connie

Converse album a few years ago has compiled an amazing collection of

19 original songs that not only confirms Molly Drake's indelible

influence on the musical output of her son, but also showcases Molly

Drake as an amazing artist and songwriter in her own right. These

intimate original songs recorded in the fifties and featuring just her

voice and piano are beautifully poignant and moving. Like the work of

Connie Converse and Sibylle Baier, they reveal a veiled melancholy in

their pure-toned and dryly heartfelt delivery as well as their charmed

combination of majestic wistfulness and simple plainsong. One can see

clearly now that Molly Drake's seemingly simple but technically

complex arrangements, sadly wise and effortlessly delivered as a key

compositional influence on the songwriting of her son. Highest

recommendation!

HIS ELECTRO BLUE VOICE "Ruthless Sperm" (Sub Pop) cd/lp 13.98/15.98

We first heard this Italian outfit on a split 7" with aQ faves

Nuit Noire, whose weirdo 'faerical blasting punk' sounded pretty much

perfect alongside the goth-stomp noise rock of His Electro Blue Voice.

So much so that we found ourselves trying to track down everything we

could. We were then thrilled to discover a His Electro Blue Voice

track on this year's Sub Pop 1000 Record Store Day sampler, a track we

described as "a gloriously twisted chunk of noisy, synthy, distorted

garagey new wave weirdness that might be worth the price of admission

alone" and which sadly is not included here, but that sound is in full

effect, gnarly, gritty, crusty, swaggery, it's actually a LOT more

aggressive and blackened than we remember, at least on opener "Death

Climb", which adds some grim black buzz to their brooding, nightmarish

punk wave gloom garage pound, but soon enough the band settle into

something a bit more tranced out, check out the new wave krautrock of

"Spit Dirt", a sprawling 8 minute motorik groove, that wraps spidery

chiming guitars and thick low slung bass chug around a seriously

hypnotic ultra propulsive rhythm, before exploding into the aggro

noise rock new wave murk and howl of "Sea Bug", that reminds us of

Iceage or the Holograms, but even more rough and ragged and

supercharged, and infused with the sort of hookiness most proper pop

bands would kill for.

After that, the band dip into some churning, thud rock pigfuck

noisiness on "Tumor" (shades of Aussie thugs Lubricated Goat, as well

as late great SF knuckle draggers Steel Pole Bathtub), with some super

cool tripped out psychedelic weirdness part way through, then some

super pop punkiness via P.I.L. on "The Path", with yet another

psychedelic breakdown, this one all synthy and shimmery, but still

driving and hypnorocky. "Born/Tired" might be the prettiest of the

bunch, the chiming intro is a dead ringer for some classic indie rock

song we can't quite put our finger on, but then the howled vox and

gristly guitars swoop in, managing to rough things up without taking

way any of the poppiness, and then ditching the buzz and crunch and

slipping into some laid back space rockiness, totally mellow and

mesmerizing. Finally, things finish off with "Red Earth", which begins

as a softly glimmering drift, all washed out thrum and psychedelic

spaciness, which is soon joined by some simple tribal drumming, hushed

vox, eventually swirls of chiming guitars add a woozy minor key feel,

as do processed synths, the song drifting into more of a dark jangle

pop, before eventually fading out, leaving just a dark shadowy swirl

of ominous synth thrum and some seriously sinister vokills, which also

fade out before you can even figure out what's going on. So cool!

Killer 'ruthless sperm' cover art too!
